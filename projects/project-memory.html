Memory Donation Center
speculative design | installation
Mar 19 - June 20

For my senior capstone project, I wanted to envision a future in which individuals could exchange and transfer memories.

Inspiration

Are first-hand experiences truly formative of one's identity? After all, we're constantly immersed in stories told by other people, lessons imparted via song and tale. If experience were the primary shaper of one's character, could a implanted memories change a person without the need of living through trauma, happiness, or conflict? I drew inspiration from famous sci-fi movies and novels like Blade Runner.

Columbia Pictures

Message

I want users to question what its like to actually live with this technology.
What would one lose if one donates a memory? Likewise, what does one gain when one receives a memory?
Moreover, I'm hoping that the artifact acts as a lens with which one could reflect on one's own identity. Are one's memories and experiences formative of their identities? Could a technology like this change our entire perception of self?

What to build

Instead of trying to make something that explictly presented my message, I wanted to build an artifact that would pose these questions by merely existing. Beyond that, I believe that immersing users within the experience would help make the concept feel more real and relateable.

Initial Idea

I wanted to build both a donation and a retrieval center, with an emphasis on the retrieval end. Users would ingest a pill, and receive a barrage of sensory inputs including light, sound, and smell.

An aspect I wanted to emphasize was the seamless union of digital and physical. While the user donating a memory fills out a questionnaire, the data is being stored digitally and linked to a physical token that is later dispensed, containing either a barcode or RFID tag. This would allow individuals on the receiving end to pick out a physical token, scan it, and begin the experience.

The challenge of immersion

I wished that a tangible component would work to further immerse the user. Being able to see leave a mark on a token, and have that be also digitally represented, and vice versa, that was my vision. At the same time, though, I needed to maintain a suspension of disbelief: the experience needed to be familiar just enough to prevent confusion, but novel and alien to the degree that users wouldn't question the realism of the technology.

I eventually discarded the retrieval side, as the light and sound show would be amateurish and abstract. The fidelity of these outputted senses would also be completely reliant on a survey meant to be kept short. Trying to capitalize on the immersion of the donation side, I played with the idea of allowing users to model clay figures representing their childhood memories. However, the disconnect between the digital and physical compenents would be jarring, and pushing around clay seemed prehistoric.

A breakthrough in plaster

While thinking of different ways a mark could be made instantaneously, in a controlled environment, while being novel, I was at a roadblock. Letting users make the mark themselves seemed too easy, so it had to be automated, yet able to be tweaked and be representative of each unique memory. Colored ink seemed to be the solution, but a token made out of paper would be too boring, and the ink would run. The ink wouldn't stand out on wood either. I just happened to have watched a documentary about Michaelangelo and his fresco painting when it hit me: plaster was the key! Ink on plaster is almost instantly absorbed, locking in the vibrancy on a pure white background. The surface of the plaster could be molded a three dimensionally, and it could also be embedded with an RFID tag. A plaster token would have a pristine, refined, yet delicate quality.

Testing different surfaces in CAD before 3D printing and casting in plaster. I'm looking for distinct ways in which the light interacts with the form.

Creating the plaster tokens

I first 3D printed and the modeled tokens. Then, I used some leftover silicone to create an open-faced mold. I wanted to make a variety at first so I'd know how the ink would splatter on the surfaces.

Within each plaster token, a unique ink splatter would be used to physically represent the embedded memory, and the RFID tag baked in would hold the unique ID attributed to the digital data. Users would describe the clarity, mood, impact, and sensation of their chosen memory, which would affect the output color, intensity, and uniformity of the droplets. The ink would be controlled by servos squeezing droppers that hold red, yellow, blue, and clear liquid.

Designing the board

The centerpiece of my installation is the donation board. The board makes storage and retrieval of the tokens easy, and serves to showcase the patterns embedded on each token.

I wanted to highlight the qualities of each material used: frosted acrylic, oak, and plaster. The wooden board is backlit, and the grooves are used as a motif throughout the installation.

Designing the kiosk

The kiosk would be the first point on interaction for users. There needed to be a space for users to fill out a questionnaire and retrieve their token; this component was crucial for linking the digital and physical representations of the memory.Users would describe the clarity, mood, impact, and sensation, which would affect the output colors, intensity, uniformity.

The questionnaire had to be short and concise, so I chose to abandon text boxes for sliders. The main feature is the memory characteristic. The complexity of the outputted ink would take a life of its own beyond the simple numerical inputs.
