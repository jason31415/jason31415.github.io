Memory Donation Center
speculative design | installation
March '19 - June '20
For my senior capstone project, I envisioned a future in which individuals could exchange and transfer memories.
Research
Inspired by sci-fi films and my own curiosity about my own sense of self, I wanted to learn more about how memories are linked with one's identity.
Motivating question
Are first-hand experiences truly formative of one's identity? After all, we're constantly immersed in stories told by other people, lessons imparted via song and tale. If experience were the primary shaper of one's character, could a implanted memories change a person without the need of living through trauma, happiness, or conflict?

Lots of reading
I dug deep into books like John Locke's An Essay on Human Understanding about the nature of human knowledge and thought, Hans Moravec's Mind Children, a speculative piece on robots and human intelligence, and even Franz Kafka's Metamorphosis.
Forming a Message
As a speculative design I wanted to paint a striking image on the future of technology, while also urging spectators to question their own nature of being.
Message
I'm urging users to question what it's like to live in a society with this technology.
Moreover, I'm hoping that the artifact acts as a lens from which one could reflect on one's own identity.
Are one's memories and experiences formative of their identities? Could a technology like this change our entire perception of self?
How to convey this message
Instead of trying to make something that explictly presented my message, the experience in its entirety should pose these questions by just existing. However, an immersive interaction should create out a more intimate and impactful experience.
Initial Proposal
My goal was to build an installation that simulate donating and retrieving memories, utilizing light, sound and smell to recreate user's dearest experiences.
Imagining that memories could be extracted and stored within pills, I had to create a convincing simulation of both the extraction and implanting process with accessible technology.
physical and digital memories
An aspect I aimed to emphasize was the seamless union of digital and physical. While the user donating a memory fills out a questionnaire, the data is being stored digitally and linked to a physical token that is later dispensed, containing either a barcode or RFID tag. Individuals on the receiving end would pick out a physical token, scan it, and experience a recreation of the memory in sound, sights, and smells.
the challenge of immersion
Having a tangible component would work to further immerse the user. Ideally, I would be able to physically leave a mark on a token, and have that also be digitally represented. Whatever medium I choose to work with, I needed to maintain a suspension of disbelief: the experience needed to be familiar just enough to prevent confusion, but novel to the degree that users wouldn't question the realism of the technology.
clay modeling
I ended up opting to just focus on the donation side, as a realistic simulation of the retrieval of a memory would be too abstract. Capitalizing on the immersion of the memory donation side, I played with the idea of allowing users to model clay figures representing their childhood memories.
Clay figurines would be attached to a base with embedded RFID tags. They could then be stored in a wall exhibiting each memory.
Working in Plaster
Plaster eventually surfaced as the mystery material that would solve my problems, promising to be of archivable, novel, and customizable.
Michelangelo's fresco in the Sinstine Chapel, its pigments locked in plaster applied over 500 years ago.
ditching clay
Sculpting clay turned out to not be the answer. It would be a nightmare to store and dispense. The fidelity of the output would depend on the user's craftsmanship, and the digital data didn't have an obvious tie to the physical component.
a breakthrough in plaster
While thinking of different ways a mark could be made instantaneously, in a controlled environment, while being novel, I was at a roadblock. Letting users make the mark themselves seemed too easy, so it had to be automated, yet have controlled parameters. I just happened to have watched a documentary about Michaelangelo and his fresco painting when it hit me: plaster was the key! Ink on plaster is almost instantly absorbed, locking in the vibrancy on a pure white background. The surface of the plaster could be molded a three dimensionally, and it could also be embedded with an RFID tag. A plaster token would have a pristine, refined, yet delicate quality.
Plaster Tokens
3D printed casts to create a silicone mold, which would then be used to cast the plaster tokens.
Testing different surfaces in CAD before 3D printing and casting in plaster, looking at the shadows and highlights of the surfaces.
creating the plaster tokens
I first 3D printed and the modeled tokens. Then, I used some leftover silicone to create an open-faced mold. I'm looking to find a surface with the most interesting splatter pattern.
Within each plaster token, a unique ink splatter would be used to physically represent the embedded memory, and the RFID tag baked in would hold the unique ID attributed to the digital data. Users would describe the clarity, mood, impact, and sensation of their chosen memory, which would affect the output color, intensity, and uniformity of the droplets. The ink would be controlled by servos squeezing droppers that hold red, yellow, blue, and clear liquid.
Designing the Installation
I needed to design the experience around retrieving and storing user memories in tokens at a kiosk, and then archiving the memories on donation board.
Designing the kiosk
The kiosk would be the first point on interaction for users. There needed to be a space for users to fill out a questionnaire and retrieve their token; this component was crucial for linking the digital and physical representations of the memory. Users would describe the clarity, mood, impact, and sensation, which would affect the output colors, intensity, uniformity.
questionnaire UI
The questionnaire would allow users to "donate" their memories by describing their characteristics, which would then be associated with the ID of the dispensed token and control the ouputted ink pattern. The questions had to be short and concise, so I chose to abandon text boxes for sliders. The complexity of the ink pattern would take a life of its own beyond the simple numerical inputs.
designing the board
The centerpiece of my installation is the donation board. The board makes storage and retrieval of the tokens easy, and serves to showcase the patterns embedded on each token.
