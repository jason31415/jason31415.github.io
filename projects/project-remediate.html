Remediate Phone Box
user research | hardware prototype
Jan '19 - Present
Chase Zheng (PM), Steve Song (PM), Xuan Jin (UI/UX), Jason Pi (ID)
Over a semester-long school project, groups were challenged to design a product to promote healthy living habits in preadolescents. Our group chose to tackle sleep and media usage as areas of our primary focus.
Research
Over the course of a semester, we worked with parents and children to develop and refine our designs.
Photo by Roland Graf
Identifying pain points
We started off by getting to know members of our target demographic: kids from the age of 8 to 12. By drafting out their daily routines, we were able to form a better understanding of the role of smart devices in their lives.
Problem
Speaking to parents, we found that a major area of conflict and stress involved managing screen time.
Starting young
It's pretty well known that using screens before bedtime can disrupt our circadians rhythms. With kids in this age group becoming early adopters of digital devices, preventing loss of sleep is especially important during this critical development period.
Conflict in the household
Multiple parents stated that deciding where to keep their child's phone at night became an area of tension. With the less obedient children putting up a fight, parents either erred on the end of being overly authoritative or lax.
Developing trust and responsibility
Speaking with the more experienced parents of the group, we learned that it was crucial to develop a sense of trust and respect when it came to dealing with these things. A different mother who worked as a rehab therapist stressed the importance of building habits through responsibility rather than fear of consequence.
Addressing the issue
We decided to design a box to store the child's device primarily before bedtime. Our design philosophy emphasized building habits by instilling responsibility rather than compulsion.
The device would be controlled by a parent's app, setting sleep and wakeup times. The phone box would then open before bed, signalling that it's bedtime, and time to put the phone away. The device would stay latched until it springs open in the morning.
Reducing reliance
By building a physical separation from the child's device, we aimed to reduce a reliance on the phone and the need to have it by one's side.
Designing the Latch
The main design feature was the electronic latch. We were forced to choose between the tradeoffs of security versus parents' peace of mind.
We wanted the experience to be rewarding and fun for the child. The lid would spring open, and sound effects would be played. The lid must also sit flush to hide any seams, preventing kids from picking and prying at the lid.
To lock or not to lock?
We teetered around the idea of locking the device. Parents felt that having the box not lock reduced the reliability, yet having it be locked made them worry about the phone being needed in emergencies. We decided to make a latching mechanism that could be opened with a push and extended hold of a button, while notifying the parent's app.
Prototype 1
The first functional iteration focused on demonstrating the latching functionality.
Using an arduino, the latching of the lid was controlled with a modified servo motor. I fitted a button underneath a spring that would be compressed by the closed lid, allowing the detection of the lid closing, and for the lid to pop up when the servo is released.
Prototype 2
After our second design review, we decided to add in a few new features. Along with the inclusion of a new parts, our overall design language had to be changed.
Adding a digital clockface to our product, the front of the box had to be angled so that the time could be easily visible. Additionally the we used a large arcade button to drive all the user interactions.
Controlled by an arduino hidden under the phone tray, the latching and night light mode could be toggled with a button. With a customizeable fabric-lined tray, the goal of this version was to create a more approachable device.
Integrating Hardware and Software
Designing the userflow for app and hardware integration.
The app allows parents to set the time period for the phone to be placed in the box. Additionally, parents can receive notifcations about the box being opened.
Next Steps
After seeing legitimate demand for our product, our group decided to keep work towards bringing our product to market. Incorporated and received over $18,000 in funding, we're currently redesigning and rebranding our product.
Currently redesigning the product with a smaller footprint fitted with a custom PCB.
