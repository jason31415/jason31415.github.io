Wobl Bobl
hardware prototype | robotics
Jan 19 - Present

Inspired by swarm robots and emotive machines, I wanted to create a pair of robots that would be able to interact without the need of human interaction.

Defining functionality

I needed to map out the inputs, outputs, and design a communication protocol that would allow the two robots to contain the code to both send and receive signals.

I was able to salvage all the materials for the body from the design studios and Walgreens. There's a paper cup to hold the electronics, sculpture wire to add support, a ping pong ball for the head, and a fuzzy sock to package it all together.

Infrared

While to decide the communication protocol, I decided to go with a completely analog method, rather than using wifi or bluetooth, This made callibration and setup a lot more straightforward.

Debugging IR issues was a hassle, though. I wouldn't know if problems were a result of a software or hardware issue, or even if the sensors were faulty to begin with. Pro tip: smoke usually means there's something wrong with the circuitry

Iteration 2

The soft body of the original robot was a little too fragile; wires came loose and it required constant readjustments. There also wasn't enough voltage to drive the bobbing mechanism while running on a 3.7 volt LiPo battery, so I decided to move on to a solid 3D printed body.

After sketching out a variety of different forms for the outer shell, I chose one that I felt would be the most feasible to 3D print and house my components.

Standardized Assembly

Building in pairs forced me to standardize my process so that it could be replicated flawlessly for both robots; this applied to both the circuitry and software.

Working with a larger footprint also meant I could add more features. The ping-pong ball was now pressable, allowing me to toggle between different settings, even a debugging mode. There was now also a speaker. Instead of bobbing their heads, I had to settle with them sending and receiving musical notes.
