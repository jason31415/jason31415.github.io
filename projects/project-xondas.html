Xondas Lamp
R&D | prototype
Mar 19 - June 20

Working with a company led by university researchers, I helped design and prototype a device meant to showcase their wireless power technology.

To display the capabilities of transmitting high wattages using inductive coupling, I was tasked to design a floating lamp.

In early designs, I looked at different ways of incorporating an interface into the product. Switches, knobs, and dials were used, ultimately detracted from the overall form.

The device had to be simple, yet stunning, and seamlessly incorporate the levitation into the design. I wanted it to be monolithic, with distinct planes and a bold silhouette.

We decided to levitate the bulb at an angle to further accentuate the floating effect. The surface directly beneath would also work to diffuse the light in a pleasant manner.

Floating Bulb

Designing the floating body was one of the toughest challenges. The bulb had to hold all the circuitry and hardware while still staying afloat. I had to constantly test and callibrate different sized bulbs throughout the prototyping phase.

Finalizing the form

After working with the actual levitating parts, I was able to finalize the size of the bulb. Working with the engineering team, we were able to shrink the footprint of the circuitboard to fit a smaller base, thus maintaining an appealing size ratio between the base and the bulb.

I eventually settled with having a main knob and a capacitive touch surface on the floating body.

Final Prototypes

Working with 3D printed parts, I added a resin coating, primer, and another layer of paint to create a matte white finish.

The entire process consisted of a multiple iterations of both the base and the floating body. Our resources allowed me to test on multiple different 3D printers and get acquainted with their capabilities and materials.
